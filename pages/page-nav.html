Page 1

The plot of Pokémon: Let's Go, Pikachu! and Let's Go, Eevee! is set in the Kanto region and includes 151 original Pokémon and their mega forms from Pokémon X and Pokémon Y, as well as Pokémon Omega Ruby and Alpha Sapphire, and Alolan forms from Pokémon Sun and Moon. In Let's Go, Pikachu! and Let's Go, Eevee!, some core elements of the main series gameplay, such as battles with non-player controlled trainers and gym leaders, are preserved. However, to capture wild Pokémon, you need to throw a Poké Ball at them (a mechanic from Pokémon Go) using Joy-Con controllers (by making throwing motions or pressing buttons and aiming if the controller is attached to the console) or a Poké Ball Plus.

The control options allow players to use only one Joy-Con, so Let's Go Pikachu and Let's Go Eevee support local multiplayer on a single screen. Shake the second Joy-Con to join in. The second player can assist the first in battles (resulting in two-on-one battles against a single opponent's Pokémon) and catch wild Pokémon, which allows for two throws of a Poké Ball, increasing the chances of capturing them.

Depending on the version, at the start of the game, the main character will receive either Pikachu or Eevee to sit on their shoulder during their journey through the Kanto region, which was first introduced in Pokémon Yellow. The companion will wave its tail when the player passes by hidden items or when the player offers them. Players can also dress up and groom their companion. The player can choose another companion Pokémon, similar to Pokémon HeartGold and SoulSilver, and even ride on certain large Pokémon, as seen in Pokémon X and Y or Pokémon Sun and Moon. Unlike previous games, in the new games, wild Pokémon are visible to the player (in previous games, they would randomly encounter the player while walking through tall grass or caves), and the player decides whether to approach them. After encountering a wild Pokémon, the battle screen shows the Pokémon, a button to feed it berries to make it easier to catch, and a Poké Ball icon (similar to Pokémon Go). The wild Pokémon available for capture vary in each version (e.g., Oddish, Sandshrew, and Growlithe can only be captured in the Pikachu version, while Bellsprout, Vulpix, and Meowth can be found in the Eevee version).

Like in all main series games, the concept of Pokémon evolution remains in Let's Go Pikachu and Let's Go Eevee. However, the initial Pikachu or Eevee obtained at the beginning of the game cannot evolve. If desired, players can capture additional Pokémon of the same species and evolve them. The breeding mechanics and hidden abilities have been removed.

Experience points, as in all main series games starting from Pokémon X and Y, can be earned not only through battles but also by capturing wild Pokémon. In Let's Go Pikachu and Let's Go Eevee, the number of experience points earned depends on the accuracy and timing of Poké Ball throws. These games introduce a feature known as "candies" that can be used to enhance the stats of Pokémon (HP, Attack, Defense, Special Attack, Special Defense, and Speed). Candies can be obtained by sending captured Pokémon to Professor Oak through the in-game storage system. The Combat Power (CP) system from Pokémon Go has been integrated into Pokémon Let's Go Pikachu and Let's Go Eevee.

Players can swap their Pokémon and engage in battles when they are locally or globally connected. The interaction system has been simplified compared to previous games in the series. For example, the Global Trade System, Wonder Trade, and Battle Spot have been removed from Pokémon Let's Go Pikachu and Let's Go Eevee. To interact with other players over the global network, a code consisting of three Pokémon is generated, and both players need to input it on their consoles to initiate trades or battles. This requires a subscription to Nintendo Switch Online. Pokémon from the first generation and their Alolan forms from Pokémon Go can be transferred to the game. They will appear in the Go Park, an in-game location where players can encounter and capture Pokémon. Pokémon with a higher CP are more challenging to catch, increasing the risk of failure when throwing Poké Balls. Some "gifts" can be transferred between games. Integration with Pokémon Go is optional.

On September 25, 2018, the developers announced that a mythical Pokémon, Meltan, a Steel-type, would be introduced in the games. On October 10, 2018, it was revealed that Meltan could not be captured without transferring a Pokémon from Pokémon Let's Go Pikachu and Let's Go Eevee to Pokémon Go.

On October 18, 2018, the developers announced the introduction of Master Trainers. These non-playable characters will appear in the game after players have defeated all members of the Elite Four. They engage in one-on-one battles using specific Pokémon species, with the restriction of using items during battles. After defeating each Master Trainer, the player will be awarded the title of Master Trainer for the species that belonged to the defeated opponent.

The Poké Ball Plus controller was specifically designed for Pokémon Let's Go Pikachu and Let's Go Eevee but is also compatible with Pokémon Go. Its use is optional. The controller has the shape of a Poké Ball, with the button on the top converted into an analog stick. The red half of the ball features a large button that does not protrude from the surface. The Poké Ball Plus can track movement, vibrate using the HD Rumble feature, emit different colors, and produce sounds of Pokémon. Players can press the analog stick to capture Pokémon instead of throwing the ball. On June 12, 2018, during a Nintendo Direct presentation at E3 2018, it was revealed that the Poké Ball Plus would contain the Pokémon Mew, which could be transferred into the game.
